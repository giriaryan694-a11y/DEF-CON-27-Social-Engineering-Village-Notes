RED TEAMING FROM DOWN UNDER

Wayne Ronaldson - DEF CON 27 Social Engineering Village
Notes By Aryan Giri

Introduction

Introduced by Chris Hadnagy, Wayne Ronaldson is noted as the **very first contestant** in the Social Engineering Capture The Flag (SECTF) 10 years prior. Chris distinguishes "real red teaming" (like Wayne's) from standard penetration testing, defining it as long-term (e.g., 90-day) engagements that target the entire business, including supply chains.

What is Red Teaming?

Wayne immediately clarifies: Red Teaming is NOT Pen Testing.

  • Penetration testing is just one small part of a red team engagement.
  • Red Teaming is a holistic view of the entire company.
  • It requires understanding the business, its competitors, its supply chain, its ethics, and its global operational differences (e.g., an office in the US vs. Australia).
  • The term "Red Teaming" is so overused and abused that Wayne now prefers the term "Adversary Simulation."

The Ideal Red Team Composition

A successful red team is built on diverse perspectives.

  • Different Life Experiences: You cannot have a team of people with the same background. They will all look at the target in the same way.
  • Dynamic Roles: Every member must be able to:
    • Work solo
    • Work in a team
    • Be a leader
    • Be led (no ego)
  • Diverse Skills: A team needs more than just hackers. Wayne has used:
    • Psychologists: "Who is the best person that knows people? A psychologist. That's their job." They help craft phishing emails and profile targets.
    • Doctors: They are not just for emergencies; they provide a completely different view of people and business processes.

Engagement: Worldwide Defense Contractor (3.5 Months)

This talk covers one part of a 3.5-month engagement.

  • Client: Worldwide Defense Contractor.
  • Goal: Simulate a real-world, global attack on the business as a whole.
  • Scope: "Whatever the adversary" (Rules of Life), with the exception of murder and kidnapping.
  • Target Focus: Executives, who are time-poor and have access to high-value business intelligence (future plans, M&A) and the supply chain.

Case Study 1: The Supply Chain Attack

The client was confident their third-party supply chain vendor, who managed their external firewalls, was secure.

  1. Wayne called the vendor's 24/7 support line at 12:30 AM.
  2. Pretext: "I'm from [client company], I need to change a firewall rule. What's the process?"
  3. The Hook: The vendor rep said, "I need an email from you and an email from your boss."
  4. Wayne immediately registered a **typosquatted domain** (very similar to the client's).
  5. He sent one email from his fake employee account and another from his fake "boss" account, CC'ing each other.
  6. Result: 20 minutes later, he had a ticket number. At 2:00 AM, he had **RDP (port 3389) opened in and out** of a major defense contractor, giving him full access to their servers from the internet.

Case Study 2: The Multi-Stage Executive Breach

Phase 1: Digital Access (The 2FA Bypass)

  • The Target: The company's single internet-facing portal: a **Citrix login with 2-Factor Authentication (2FA)**.
  • The Attack: A team member built a tool to intercept and **bypass the 2FA**.
  • A phishing email ("critical patches to Intel processors") was sent to just three executives. One clicked.
  • Key Finding: The 2FA bypass was so effective that it **wrote 0 logs to their 25-person SOC**.

Phase 2: Business Intelligence (The Calendar)

  • Once inside, Wayne goes to two places:
    1. The "Welcome Pack": Found by scrolling to the *bottom* of an inbox. It contains portals, links, and sometimes even credentials.
    2. The Calendar: This is the most important.
  • The Find: A calendar entry for a 12-executive, 2-day off-site meeting to discuss the company's 10-year plan.
  • The SE/Physical Flaw: The execs held the meeting at a restaurant (with wine and beer) because the "food tastes like crap" at their secure facility, breaking all corporate policies.

Phase 3: Physical Recon & Social Engineering

  1. Wayne knew all 12 executives' faces from their Outlook profile pictures.
  2. He called the restaurant. Pretext 1: "I'm looking to book a 12-person room. Can I come see it?"
  3. Response: "That room is booked for a private, confidential meeting on those dates."
  4. Pretext 2: "No problem, I'm in town anyway. Can I just come by and have lunch, and poke my head in the room while they're at lunch just to take a photo for my boss?"
  5. He arrived, waited for the execs to go downstairs, and a staff member escorted him up to the room.
  6. Pretext 3 (Re-entry): He pretended to take a selfie by accident. He ran back to the front desk, "So sorry, I took a photo of myself, my boss will kill me. Can I just run up and be real quick?"
  7. The staff member let him go up **alone**.

Phase 4: The "Jackpot" (Unsupervised Access)

In the room, Wayne found:

  • 12 executive laptops, all open and unlocked.
  • The main presentation laptop, also unlocked.
  • Handwritten notes from the 10-year planning meeting all over the desk.
  • Multiple USB sticks.

In 15-20 minutes, he "owned all 12" laptops, took photos of all the notes, copied all the USBs, and planted implants on them.

Phase 5: The USB Drop

  • The execs had 4-hour parking and had to come down to move their cars.
  • Wayne watched one exec re-park his car and dropped a malicious USB stick right by the driver's side door.
  • Result: The executive picked it up and plugged it in. "It's really silly that this still works."

Demo: Overwatch Offensive

Wayne demoed his custom tool, "Overwatch Offensive."

  • It is NOT a replacement for tools like Cobalt Strike.
  • Purpose: It is used for special, high-value targets to gather **long-term business intelligence**.
  • It does not move laterally.
  • It uses extremely long callback times (48-72 hours or more) to evade detection.
  • The demo showed it can take screenshots and, most notably, play audio recordings captured from the target's microphone.

Rules of Engagement (from Q&A)

  • Client Contact: Wayne is in **100% constant, live contact** with a trusted agent inside the organization, telling them exactly what is happening as it happens.
  • Police: If approached by law enforcement (not a security guard), he will try his pretext *once*. If they question him a second time, he **"lets the cat out of the bag" immediately.**
  • Priority #1 is safety: Safety of his team, himself, and the client.
