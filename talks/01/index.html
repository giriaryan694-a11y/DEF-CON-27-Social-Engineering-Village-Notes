DEF CON 27 Social Engineering Village: R. Paul Wilson

Notes By Aryan Giri

Introduction: An Impromptu Talk

R. Paul Wilson, host of the BBC's "The Real Hustle," gives an impromptu talk about his life and experiences in the world of cons, scams, and magic. He notes that his fascination with both began at age eight. He jokes that life (perhaps a simulation) "allowed me to perform more cons and scams than any other human being in history," not because he's the best, but because his unique career path had him demonstrating them constantly for research and entertainment.

The "Gang Who Couldn't Cheat Straight"

Wilson explains his path to TV, starting with consulting on a movie about cheating starring Sylvester Stallone. This led to a reality show called "The Takedown," where he and a team were allowed by the Gaming Control Board to cheat in real casinos (with their permission).

They called themselves "the gang who couldn't cheat straight" because it was often their first time trying these complex cheats in a live environment. This experience and his expertise led directly to his job on the BBC's "The Real Hustle."

Case Study 1: The Glasgow Poker Game (A Lesson Learned)

Wilson recounts an early story from before his TV career, playing in the "Jim Baxter game" in Glasgow, a very tough town.

  • The game used cards that were so old they were already marked from wear. Wilson eventually supplied new decks to the game, which he had secretly marked himself.
  • He played heads-up in a tournament against "Steve M," a world-class player and a world-class jerk.
  • With a crowd watching, Wilson peeked at the top card and saw it would give him a flush. He decided to perform a "second deal" (a high-level sleight-of-hand move) to deal the second card to his opponent and keep the flush card for himself.
  • He executed the move perfectly, went all-in with his flush... and his opponent beat him with a full house (made from the second card).
  • The Lesson: Wilson realized his cheat backfired. He states that if he had played honestly, he would have received that second card himself (which he claims "was *also* that card I needed for the flush") and he would have won.

Methodology of "The Real Hustle"

Wilson stresses that, despite what people may think, they absolutely scammed those people for real. The victims were not actors.

  • The "trick" of the show was the very short time between the person realizing they'd lost their money and the crew stepping in.
  • The crew would capture that moment of genuine shock before revealing the con.
  • The "magic line" that defused every single situation: "It's okay, I'm with the BBC."

Case Study 2: Authority and De-escalation

The Fluorescent Jacket Scam

  • In Europe, a yellow fluorescent jacket grants instant, automatic authority.
  • They put an "Out of Order" sign on a parking meter that read, "Please give the cash to the guy with a..." (him, in the jacket).
  • The scam was *so successful* they ran out of "runners" to return the money to the victims.

The Camera Lens Theft

  • In Edinburgh, his partner "Polly" would distract a tourist with a map held over their camera lens.
  • Wilson would reach under the map, twist the lens off the camera body, and pocket it.
  • One time, the timing was off, and the tourist (a large man) saw Wilson holding his lens. The man grabbed his heavy-duty camera, ready to "bury it" in Wilson's face.
  • Wilson's response: "It's okay, I'm with the BBC." The man instantly froze, the crew emerged, and the situation was completely de-escalated.

Case Study 3: "The Bouncer" Scam (Victim Psychology)

This scam was pulled directly from an old con artist's booklet.

  1. Step 1: Sell a box of pens for 5 pounds. Get a crowd. Ask, "Who's happy?" They raise their hands.
  2. Step 2: "If they're happy, give them their money back!" They return all the money.
  3. Step 3: Sell a piece-of-junk glass vase for 10 pounds. More people buy. "Who's happy?"
  4. Step 4: "Give them their money back!" They return all the money again.
  5. Step 5: Sell a junk watch for 50 pounds. Everyone buys. "Who's happy?" The crowd shouts, "We're happy!"
  6. Step 6: "Well, if you're happy, we're happy!" Wilson shuts the truck shutter and they drove away.

The Twist: When the crew went back to return the 50 pounds, most of the victims didn't want to give the junk bag back. They weren't angry they got scammed; they were unhappy the scam *stopped*. They had seen PlayStations and TVs in the back of the truck and assumed the game would continue until they got those.

Case Study 4: The Celebrity Scam (Improvisation)

  • The show brought on a celebrity, Caprice, to pass counterfeit money.
  • The Scam: Buy a cheap item with a 50-pound note. Get change (e.g., two 20s). Switch the real 20s for counterfeit 20s and say, "Actually, do you have tens?" The cashier, believing the money just came from their own till, exchanges it without checking.
  • The Failure: Caprice fumbles the move. The cashier spots the *other* counterfeit notes under the DVDs and is about to call the police. Wilson's partner, Jess, immediately bails and leaves the store.
  • The "Social Engineer": Wilson and his co-host Alex (in ridiculous suits) walk into the shop. Wilson declares, "Police. We've been following these two girls who've been passing off counterfeit cash."
  • The cashier was overjoyed. They not only "arrested" Caprice and got her out, but they also "confiscated" the *other* bad money from the cashier's till as "evidence."

Favorite Scam 1: "The Razzle" (An Unwinnable Game)

Wilson's all-time favorite scam is "The Razzle," a carnival game that you cannot possibly win.

  • The Game: You roll marbles into a box with numbered holes. The numbers are added up. If your total is on the scoreboard (e.g., 44, 35, 24), you win points. Get 10 points, you win the big prize (a car, TV, etc.).
  • The Secret: The numbers on the board are statistically impossible (or "billions to one") to hit. You can only get a winning score if the operator cheats on your behalf by miscounting the total.
  • The Hook: One number on the board (e.g., 29) has no points. Instead, it says "HP" (House Price). If you hit this, you must double your bet to continue. The operator also "doubles the prizes."
  • The Con: The operator "helps" you win, letting you get to 9 or 9.5 points. Then, they make sure you hit the "HP" number. Your bet doubles (from $2 to $4, $8, $16, $32...).
  • The "Close": You run out of money. The operator says, "I've never seen this happen! You're so close. I'll hold your score for 10 minutes. Go to the ATM, get $400, and I'll give you 20 rolls to get that last half-point."
  • The End: The victim *always* goes. They come back, roll 20 times themselves, and *never win*â€”because the operator is no longer "miscounting" to help them. They are so psychologically invested and "certain" they will win that they cannot stop.

Favorite Scam 2: Three-Card Monte

Wilson demonstrates Three-Card Monte (two Jokers, one Queen).

  • He explains the basic sleight-of-hand move (a "midair switch").
  • The Real Secret: The con isn't just the move. If the operator "gets a smell" that you know how the move works, they will simply... stop cheating.
  • Even if you mark the card (like putting a bend in it), you will lose. The operator will take the bend out or transfer it. It's a game of "theatrical mugging" you cannot win.

Conclusion: Practical Sleight of Hand for Security

Wilson concludes by teasing his 2020 workshop. He plans to apply these sleight-of-hand principles to real-world security and social engineering.

  • The goal is not to target people, but to have the tools to protect your own information.
  • Example: If someone in authority demands your camera's SD card to be "confiscated," he can teach you how to palm it and switch in a dummy card during the action of handing it over.
  • This includes managing situations, hiding items (USB sticks, laptops), and using distraction the way a magician does to gain an advantage.
