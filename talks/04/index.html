Ryan MacDougall - DEF CON 27 Social Engineering Village

Introduction: The Mindset, Not the Tools

Ryan MacDougall, a social engineer and OSINT trainer, clarifies that this talk isn't about client-based, high-level OSINT. Instead, it's about using **basic, practical OSINT skills** in everyday life.

  • His core philosophy: "It's not the toolset, it's the mindset."
  • He emphasizes the importance of **"natural observation"**â€”being aware of your surroundings and the unseen information available.
  • The goal is to use OSINT for "fun stories, freaking out your neighbors, and getting the edge in any job."

Case Study 1: The Truck Books (Fun with a 5-Year-Old)

  • Goal: A fun game with his son to find the origin of trucks pictured in his library books.
  • Method:
    • They find phone numbers and license plates in the book's photos.
    • Using a phone, they search the area code (or full number) to find the company's location (e.g., St. Paul, MN; Michigan).
    • They then plot the locations on his son's US map.
  • Key Takeaway: He's teaching his son to see *all* the information on a page, not just the text. For example, by looking closer at one truck, they could read "Eagan, Minnesota" directly on the door, confirming their earlier search.

Case Study 2: Vetting the Neighbors (Victor & Julia)

  • Goal: He and his wife (both introverts) kept forgetting their neighbors' names and wanted to vet them as "fine upstanding people" to be around their son.
  • The OSINT Path:
    1. Used **Google Maps** to get the house address.
    2. Searched the address to find **Public Voter Records**.
    3. Voter records provided three names: **Victor** (the father's name he remembered) and an "assumed wife's" name, which he didn't recognize.
    4. The records also showed a **business registered** at the address.
    5. A search for the business confirmed the "assumed wife's" name as the owner and led to a **Facebook page**.
    6. Facebook confirmed their photos and listed the university she attended.
    7. The Problem: The name on Facebook and the business records was *still not* the name he remembered hearing.
    8. He followed another lead (Spokeo) for Victor. The "Related To" section listed *both* the business name *and* the name he thought he knew: **"Julia."**
    9. A new Google search for "Julia [Last Name]" + State led to a **LinkedIn profile** for a Vice President of a bank (with no photo).
    10. The Correlation: The education on this new LinkedIn profile matched the education listed on the original Facebook page.
    11. A **Google Image search** for "Julia [Last Name]" found a picture of her, which linked back to the *same* LinkedIn profile. (He notes: "Google never forgets").
  • Conclusion: He confirmed her real name ("Julia") and her high-level job at a bank, making him feel more comfortable.

Case Study 3: The Contractor (Dan)

  • Goal: Vet a contractor for a home project.
  • Starting Info: Only a first name ("Dan") and a phone number.
  • The OSINT Path:
    1. A phone number search led to a "home builder company" name.
    2. Searching the company + "Dan" led to a Manta.com link, which provided his **full last name**.
    3. Another link for the company was dead, but he used **Google Cache** to view the old page.
    4. The cached page was **Dan's resume!**
    5. The resume showed his career history: former Chef, then HVAC tech, then home builder.
    6. A new search for his full name revealed he was also the co-owner of a **cannabis business**.
  • Conclusion: The OSINT raised *more questions* than it answered. Why did he start a new construction company this year? Was he spread too thin? He was left unsure about hiring him.

Core Lesson: Knowledge is Confidence

Ryan's main point is that "knowledge is power" is better said as **"knowledge is confidence."** The more you know about your target, client, or even neighbor, the more confident and effective you can be.

Q&A Highlights

  • Favorite Tools: Search Engines (Google, Baidu, Yandex). He stresses that the **mindset and documentation are key**, as tools come and go.
  • Data Storage: He uses a simple, clear documentation method:
    1. Line 1: The exact search query.
    2. Line 2: The URL of the useful result.
    3. Line 3: The specific tidbits of information found.
  • Paywall Sites (e.g., People.com): He **rarely** uses them. He consciously *avoided* them for this talk and found he got a *better* and less overwhelming story by sticking to public information.
  • Protecting Your Own Privacy: You can't do much. The most important thing is to **know what is publicly available about you** and "react accordingly" when someone uses that information.
  • Avoiding Rabbit Holes: "Doing OSINT with no goal is just web surfing." Always have a specific goal. If you go 3-5 steps down a path and find nothing useful, pull back and try a different fork.
