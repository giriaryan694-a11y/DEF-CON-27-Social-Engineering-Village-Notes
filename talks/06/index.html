Leveraging the Insider Threat (And How to Be Awesome)

Marcus Liotta - DEF CON 27 Social Engineering Village
Notes By Aryan Giri

Introduction

The talk begins with a clarification: you can't teach everyone "to be awesome," but you can **"teach people how to believe you are awesome."** This builds confidence and motivational value.

The Modern Threat Landscape (Statistics)

Marcus outlines a massive increase in threats from 2016-2018, citing Symantec and Verizon reports.

  • 55% of all email is still considered spam (phishing, ads, junk).
  • 600% increase in attacks on IoT devices.
  • 1000% increase in PowerShell-based malware attacks.
  • 1 in 10 emails scanned at corporate gateways is malicious.
  • 7,700 organizations are hit by Business Email Compromise (BEC) scams *every month*.
  • 71% of attacks are spearfishing (targeted, information-backed attacks).

On-Location & Future Threats

  • Wi-Fi Replication: Creating fake Wi-Fi gateways to steal credentials (rogue APs).
  • Mobile Hijacking: Malicious apps (like a fake "flappy bird") that steal banking info or perform cryptojacking.
  • Drones: Using drones to create mobile, malicious Wi-Fi portals.
  • Real-World Example: In June (2019), **NASA was hacked** via an unauthorized Raspberry Pi (an IoT device) connected to their network. The attacker stole moon-landing data.

The One Common Factor: People

The single common factor behind all these threats is **people**. People are the intelligence behind every attack.

Psychological Manipulation Tactics (Effectiveness)

An analysis of which social engineering tactics actually work.

  • Ineffective: Threats & Abuse. Threatening someone ("you're gonna lose your job") makes them angry and uncooperative.
  • Ineffective: Reason-Based Tactics. People are fundamentally "lazy." If you give them a reason that requires them to do extra work, they "don't care" and will "buzz you off."
  • Effective: Charm Tactics. Joking and being pleasant makes people enjoy your company and want to help.
  • Effective: Mutually Beneficial. This is why "Prince" scams (greed) worked so well. "Do a small favor, get $10,000."

Attack & Manipulation Techniques

  • The "One Shot" Rule: You have one shot to build trust. If you explore too many tactics and fail, that person will no longer trust you.
  • Lying: Marcus argues *against* lying.
    • It's not the most effective option.
    • It's **"very difficult to maintain a lie"** over time.
    • It breaks relationships and long-term trust (bad for an attacker wanting persistent access).
  • Lying by Omission: Not telling the whole truth (e.g., the dog not telling you it ate the trash).
  • Rationalization: Playing dumb to justify an action. ("Oh, I didn't know I needed an ID card, I'm just cleaning up...").
  • Guilt Trip: Making a failure your fault. ("I couldn't do my job because the report *you* gave me got thrown out...").
  • Servant Role: Using a position of (often fake) authority. ("Dispatch said we've got to take you in...").
  • Bandwagon Effect: "Everybody does it." This is a major risk in corporate culture ("Jake did the same thing," "IT needs your password, I gave him mine...").

Prevention: Leveraging the (Good) Insider

This section covers the *defensive* side: how to leverage your employees (the insiders) to build a strong defense.

  • Lock your computer. Enforce screen lock timeouts.
  • Train users to be suspicious. Assume callers and emails *could* be fraudulent.
  • Use **external email flagging** on your mail gateway.
  • Conduct **quarterly training**.
  • Train users to **verify a caller's identity** *before* revealing sensitive info.
  • Define **proper, clear reporting procedures** so users know who to contact.

How to Be Awesome: Leveraging Your *Own* Influence

This is the *offensive* (personal influence) side of the talk: how to make people *believe* you are awesome.

  • Be Bold: Be the person in the room who "raises their hand" and has the answer.
  • Mirroring: Mirror behavior and emotions. People like people who are like them.
  • Tactic: Repeated Wordplay.
    • As an IT guy, Marcus would fix a problem and joke, "No problem, I'm just awesome like that."
    • After 27 days, "dozens of people" in the company started saying it *for him*: "Oh, Marcus? Yeah, he's just awesome like that."
  • Tactic: The Restaurant Review (How to get free sushi).
    1. Go to a restaurant and socialize with your server. Get their name.
    2. Leave a 5-star review (e.g., on Google) and **use their name** in the review.
    3. Show the server the review. Show their *manager* the review.
    4. When you come back, they will remember you. After a few times, "you're going to start getting free stuff."
  • Key Philosophy: Users are NOT the Enemy. If you work in IT or programming, do not "crush their rebellion." Help them.
  • Teamwork: Rely on your friends and team. Everyone has an attribute you do not have.
