Why Vigilantism Doesn't Work

Shane McCombs - DEF CON 27 Social Engineering Village
Notes By Aryan Giri

Introduction: Stirring Thought, Not Debate

Shane McCombs clarifies his goal is not to start a debate about whether vigilantism is good or bad. The goal is to "get our thinking stirred up" and move people to effective action.

  • Vigilantism ("law enforcement undertaken without legal authority") appeals to our core sense of justice.
  • When we see an injustice, our "internal sense of justice is offended." This is the same principle as reciprocity, and it's why heroes like Batman are so successful.

The Core Problem: Why Vigilantes Exist

  • The Statistic: A "realistic" and "unacceptable" statistic is that **1 in 10 children** will experience sexual abuse before their 18th birthday. He notes this is based on *reported* numbers, so the real number is "probably much higher."
  • The Systemic Failure: When families go to the system for help, they find good people who are **"understaffed, underfunded, overworked"** and can't keep up.
  • This "gap" is what vigilante predator hunter groups are attempting to fill.

Predator Hunting 101 (The Common Vigilante Method)

  1. Pretend to be a minor on social media.
  2. Build a relationship with anyone who interacts (casting a "wide net").
  3. If the relationship turns sexual, cultivate it.
  4. Arrange an in-person meeting.
  5. Record or livestream the confrontation, where the person is "confronted, named, shamed, humiliated."

The 8 Key Challenges of Vigilantism (Why It Doesn't Work)

This is the core of the argument. The vigilante method has fundamental, often counter-productive, flaws.

  1. Physical Safety: The confrontations are dangerous. More importantly, if the predator is shamed and *not* arrested, what might they do when they go home to a child they have access to?
  2. Failed Prosecutions (Capability): He played a clip of a lawyer stating that prosecutions **fail** because the hunters "went too far," and the court cannot prove the person would have committed the crime without the hunter's actions.
  3. Legality (Entrapment):** This is a huge legal debate that taints the cases.
  4. Police Conflicts: Law enforcement is now being told to "crack down on the vigilante hunter group." The Michigan Attorney General issued a "cease and desist" because they were **"jeopardizing police work."**
  5. Hunters Get Sued: Predators are now turning around and *suing* the hunter groups.
  6. Drains Resources: Law enforcement's already-strained resources are now split between catching the *real* criminals and managing the vigilantes.
  7. The Wrong Focus: Vigilantism focuses almost exclusively on the "stranger danger" threat. Statistics overwhelmingly show that most abuse comes from **family members and acquaintances**.
  8. Educating the Enemy: By "going full-on open kimono" and showing their methods on livestreams, they are **"teaching these guys how to avoid them"** (and law enforcement) in the future.

A Better Way: The Innocent Lives Foundation (ILF) Framework

Shane proposes a "different, non-vigilante framework" that is designed to specifically bypass the challenges listed above.

  • Principle 1: Anonymity & No Confrontation: This is the *opposite* of the vigilante model. It eliminates the physical safety risk, removes the legal threat to the hunters, and stops them from "teaching the predators."
  • Principle 2: Trained & Qualified Members:** Only approved team members can interact, minimizing the chance of "failure" (i.e., failed prosecutions).
  • Principle 3: Target "Confirmed Predators":** Instead of casting a "wide net," they target those *already* "misbehaving... in public space" (breaking the law). This **"bypasses the questions of legality and entrapment."**
  • Principle 4: Cultivate Law Enforcement (LE) Relationships: They actively partner with LE, submitting information through a "predefined and approved process." They become a force multiplier *for* the system, not an obstacle to it.

ILF Operations & Call to Action

The ILF is run as a non-profit business, not a vigilante group, with SOPs, a Board of Directors, and a **mandatory wellness program** because the work is so "hard" ("rolling in muck all day long").

  • Is it scalable? Yes. They are building SOPs to "stamp it out." They went from 9 cases submitted to LE last year to **63 cases** this year. "It's a little dent, but you got to start somewhere."
  • Working with NCMEC? Not directly, but they have working relationships and are taking advice.
  • Team Size: 26 people total (including 6 on the board and 11 in the "pit," or Predator Identification Team).
